# Peer Review: "From Persons to Families to Organizations: A Cross-Level CAS Foundation for Brief Health Screening"

*Review date: June 12, 2026. Method: multi-agent review across seven lenses (CAS theory, family science, psychometrics/multilevel measurement, OHA application, literature coverage, structure/originality, adversarial), with every major concern verified against the manuscript text and 30 empirical/historical claims fact-checked against the literature. Severity calls below reflect post-verification calibration.*

---

## 1. Executive summary of the paper's main contribution

The paper argues that the proposal to build a brief organizational health screener on the model of clinical instruments like the PHQ-9 — previously advanced in the authors' own carr2025 paper — is vulnerable as a single-leap analogy because it crosses two levels of analysis at once (one actor to many; self-reported internal state to system-referent emergent property). Its remedy is to decompose the leap into two steps via family systems, which it presents as an already-occupied intermediate level: family systems theory anticipated core CAS constructs, and family assessment (FAD, SCORE-15) built validated, multidomain, multi-informant, repeatable brief instruments for an emergent relational construct. From the clinical and family precedents the paper derives a cross-level measurement architecture — a five-element assessment-system abstraction, construct correspondence tables, and multilevel measurement foundations (referent-shift and dispersion composition, aggregation discipline, role-stratified distortion analysis) — with an explicit transfer analysis (transfers / adapts / does not transfer) and the OHA as worked application. Claims are bounded throughout: the architecture supplies design rationale, not validity, which remains an organizational-level empirical obligation addressed by a staged companion program (LLM-based known-answer calibration at lower levels, then pilot and field validation).

The genuinely novel kernel, post-scrutiny, is narrower than the title's framing but real: (1) family **measurement instruments** (not family theory) as design precedents for organizational screening — prior art transferred theory and metaphor, not instrument architecture; (2) the staged known-answer calibration design, where the family level does unique, non-decorative work that neither the person level nor the team literature can do; (3) the explicit element-by-element transfer discipline, which is exactly what most analogical-transfer papers lack.

## 2. Overall assessment and publication recommendation

**Recommendation: Major revision.** All seven review lenses converged independently on this disposition. No concern found is fatal: the modest version of the thesis — family assessment as a validated feasibility precedent for the person-to-multi-actor measurement transition, plus an explicit transfer architecture and a calibration resource — survives every correction identified below. But four load-bearing claims currently say more than the argument delivers, the measurement plan has a real gap (small-N psychometrics; the reliability half of aggregation), several adjacent literatures that bound the paper's originality are unengaged, and there are citation-integrity errors that must be fixed before any submission. The required revisions are reframings, epistemic-status corrections, and citations — not new research — which is why this is major revision rather than reject or revise-and-resubmit.

The paper's claim discipline is genuinely above the genre norm and pre-absorbs the worst version of most objections; the central problem is that the headline framing (abstract, contribution statements, Figure 1 caption, conclusion) repeatedly asserts the strong form of claims that the body carefully hedges. Aligning the headlines with the body would resolve perhaps half the major concerns by itself.

## 3. Major strengths

1. **Explicit as-CAS claim discipline (§2.1).** "An as-CAS claim, as used here, asserts only that a system instantiates the structural property set above… What transfers must then be established property by property." Most cross-domain CAS papers never state this standard; this one states it and mostly honors it.
2. **The transfer analysis (Table 4, §3.4) is the paper's best asset.** Classifying each design element under a single explicit rule, with substantive "does not transfer" rows (content, norms/cutoffs, validity evidence) and the correct Kane/Standards rationale, preempts the standard abuse of cross-domain instrument arguments.
3. **Genuine epistemic bookkeeping in §3.1**: the indicator-sampling claim and the early-warning claim are explicitly split by evidential weight, and organizational early warning is twice downgraded to "a motivating hypothesis rather than an established finding."
4. **The architecture has demonstrable bite on the authors' own instrument** (§4.4): the published OHA items "mix self-referent wordings ('my responsibilities') with team- and organization-referent wordings," and referent alignment is prescribed as a pre-freeze revision. A purely rationalizing paper would not have flagged this.
5. **§3.3's families-vs-teams treatment is fair**, with five explicit criteria, genuine credit to team research (TCI, TDS, referent-shift practice), and a complementarity rather than exclusion conclusion.
6. **Governance (§4.5) rises above boilerplate**: purpose drift named as "a live failure mode"; results returned to the assessed unit, not exclusively leadership; the insight that governance commitments are "psychometric as well as ethical."
7. **Limitations are substantive and falsifiable** — Limitation 5's boundary conditions (dense interdependence, member observability, compositional stability) name concrete out-of-scope populations (matrixed structures, gig workforces).
8. **Scholarship within the chosen verticals checks out.** Fact-checking confirmed the Jackson/Minuchin/Bowen/Watzlawick/Cox-Paley attributions, the SCORE facts, the Chan/James/LeBreton/Bliese/Putnick/Kane deployments, the Davidz pathology categories, and the LLM-simulation literature characterizations.

## 4. Major concerns requiring revision

None of these is fatal. They are ordered by importance; items A–D are conditions of acceptance.

**A. The family-level "brief-plus-comprehensive pairing" is partly an anachronistic re-description, and the "independent re-emergence" induction overstates the history.** *(Confirmed; raised independently by five of seven lenses.)* The pattern's definitional element at the clinical level — "definitionally for screening, the brief instrument is validated against a comprehensive reference assessment" (§3.1), where the comparator (SCID) is independent in method, administrator, and validation history — is not what the family level exhibits. The FAD-GF is a nested short form (shared method, same sitting, part-whole relation); SCORE-40 was the developmental item pool from which SCORE-15 was distilled (via an intermediate ~28-item form the paper omits) and was never administered as a comprehensive comparator. The true within-tradition independent comparators — the McMaster Clinical Rating Scale and the McMaster Structured Interview of Family Functioning — go unmentioned. Meanwhile: (i) brief screeners validated against comprehensive psychiatric assessment long predate the FAD (BDI 1961, Zung 1965, Goldberg's GHQ 1972 — validated against the Clinical Interview Schedule), so "cannot be a derivative imitation of the clinical exemplar" rebuts only the PHQ-9, not the tradition the McMaster psychiatrists worked inside; (ii) SCORE is by name and lineage an adaptation of the individual-level CORE-OM routine-outcome-monitoring system (Chris Evans, CORE-OM's developer, co-authored the paper's own cited stratton2014) — a *deliberate cross-level transfer*, not an independent re-emergence; (iii) the FAD-GF's standalone validation is Byles, Byrne, Boyle & Offord (1988, *Family Process*) in the Ontario Child Health Study — which actually *supports* the priority claim (13 years before the PHQ-9) but is uncited; Boterhoven de Haan 2015 validates the further-shortened GF6+ and cannot carry the weight currently placed on it. **Fix:** reframe "two independent instances… are evidence of a level-general solution" (§3.2) as an existence proof plus shifted burden (which §3.2's own closing already half-states); cite Byles 1988; re-describe SCORE's CORE-OM lineage — a documented successful individual-to-system transfer is arguably *better* evidence for your thesis than independence; relabel the family "Comprehensive reference" column (Table 3, Figure 1) or substitute the MCRS/McSiFF; soften the conclusion's hardened "brief forms paired with comprehensive comparators, two decades before the clinical screening exemplar existed."

**B. The OHRA cannot do the definitional work the pattern assigns to the comprehensive comparator, and the paper never names this asymmetry as a disanalogy.** *(Confirmed.)* At the lower rungs the comparator carries independent, accumulated validity evidence; the OHRA is unvalidated, assembled by the same program "from public-domain descriptions" of proprietary frameworks, sharing method and respondents with the OHA. OHA–OHRA convergence is therefore weak extrapolation evidence (substantially guaranteed by shared method and construct space), yet §5.3 names it "the relationship that defines screening at every level of the ladder," and Table 3/Figure 1 give the OHRA visual parity with SCID and the full FAD. The "not a gold standard" disclaimer names the problem without analyzing its consequences, and none of the seven limitations covers the OHRA — a conspicuous gap in a paper that scrutinizes the *family* precedents' weaknesses (Limitation 3). To be fair (verification): the paper never claims OHA–OHRA convergence validates the OHA; criterion evidence (retention, delivery, incidents) already sits in the same extrapolation layer; and the family rung itself was not method-independent — the unique disanalogy is the OHRA's absent validity evidence, not method overlap per se. **Fix:** name the comparator-provenance asymmetry explicitly (a sentence in §2.5 or an eighth limitation); annotate Table 3/Figure 1 (e.g., "constructed, unvalidated"); state what OHA–OHRA convergence can and cannot show, citing podsakoff2003 at this juncture; elevate independent criterion evidence to co-primary status in extrapolation; document the OHRA's construction and content-validity procedure at least in summary (it is as load-bearing as the OHA and receives ~three sentences).

**C. Small-N psychometrics: the §4.3 evaluation plan omits the reliability half of aggregation and is unstable or undefined at the unit sizes the paper targets.** *(Confirmed.)* The paper cites Bliese (2000) — whose central contribution is the agreement-vs-reliability distinction — yet ICC(1)/ICC(2) appear nowhere ("reliability" does not occur in the body text); ICC(2) depends directly on group size and threatens exactly the trajectory-monitoring use case the paper makes primary. r_wg(j) is unstable with few raters and its uniform-null assumption is contestable for all-positively-keyed items. Role-stratified internal consistency and invariance testing across frontline/supervisor/executive are undefined for cells of one and conventionally need ~100+ per group; pooling executives across units confounds unit and role and violates independence; none of the small-sample remedies (multilevel CFA, alignment, approximate invariance) is named. Internal inconsistency: §4.5 fixes minimum cell sizes for *privacy* but the plan never fixes them for *estimability*, though the latter binds first. Relatedly, a six-scale profile from ~20 items (3–4 per scale) has **no precedent at any rung the paper invokes** — the McMaster six dimensions live in a 60-item comprehensive instrument; the brief forms on the ladder collapse to a single scale (GF-12) or three 5-item subscales (SCORE-15) — and "the same coverage-versus-burden balance that fixed the McMaster model at six dimensions" mischaracterizes epstein1978 (the dimensions were theory-derived, not burden-derived; the paper's own §2.3 says so). Change scores from 3-item scales at small respondent counts will be noise-dominated. **Fix:** add a small-sample paragraph to §4.3 (ICC(1)/(2) alongside r_wg; null-distribution justification; minimum rater counts for reportable aggregates; cross-unit pooling via multilevel CFA with the between-unit structure as the focal test; alignment methods for sparse roles); either adopt GF-logic for the brief tier (general index plus domain flags) or explicitly defend the 6×3 profile with unit-level reliability targets and a limitation acknowledging it has no ladder precedent; delete or rewrite the McMaster burden sentence; add small-N psychometrics as an eighth limitation.

**D. Citation-integrity errors that must be fixed before submission.** *(Fact-checked.)* (i) The `thombs2021` bib entry is wrong in attribution, author list, and title: BMJ 2021;375:n2183 is **Negeri et al. 2021** (Thombs is senior/last author), and the bib's author list (Engel, Gershon, Henry, Ivis, Kwakkenbos, Vigod…) contains names that are not authors of that paper — this has the signature of a fabricated/AI-generated entry and journals treat such errors harshly. The updated IPD-MA estimates are 0.85/0.85 at cutoff ≥10 (Levis et al. 2019 BMJ reports 0.88/0.85), so "broadly sustained" is defensible but cite correctly and ideally give the updated numbers. (ii) The FAD as published in **Epstein et al. 1983 had 53 items**, not 60; the 60-item version is a later revision (cite Kabacoff et al. 1990 or Miller et al. 2000 for the current count). (iii) The `camp2024` author list is wrong: the McKinsey Quarterly piece is by Camp, Gast, Goldstein & Weddle. (iv) Audit the entire .bib against publisher records before submission; also remove or cite the never-cited `carrPracticumProposalPresentation2026` entry.

**E. "The measurement-theoretically hard transitions are already solved" asserts a hardness ordering that is never argued.** *(Confirmed, low-end major.)* Figure 1's caption, §4.1, and the conclusion ("shows the hardest ones already solved") rank person-to-family adjustments as harder than family-to-organization ones, but no criterion is given, and the supporting argument is symmetric and therefore non-discriminating: the new demands are tractable because "organizational measurement theory has tools to offer" — yet the person-to-family demands were tractable because family assessment had tools too. A skeptic can reverse the ordering: family informants observe substantially the whole intimate system, face no formal power gradient, and number 2–6; organizational informants observe role-local slices under career consequences for candor — conditions on which the family precedent is, by the paper's own §2.5 concession, silent. The family rung partially *relocates* rather than reduces inferential risk. **Fix:** conditionalize — the person-to-multi-actor transitions have been made once, under near-complete shared observability, minimal power differentials, and small informant sets; their robustness under role-stratified partial observation is the organizational-level open question (cross-reference §§4.3, 4.5, which already contain the machinery). Provide a criterion for the ordering or drop it. Relatedly, drop "nested" (abstract, Table 1 caption, conclusion): organizations do not contain families; the ladder spans two compositional hierarchies ordered by measurement-relevant properties, and one clarifying sentence to that effect costs nothing.

**F. The missing literatures that bound the paper's originality.** *(Confirmed for homology and the assessment-design frameworks; the rest verified as genuine omissions.)* (i) **Multilevel construct homology** — Chen, Bliese & Mathieu (2005, ORM) is the established framework for stating and testing exactly the cross-level correspondence hypotheses of Table 5, and "homolog*" occurs zero times in the manuscript; an ORM-class reviewer will treat this as disqualifying as written. (ii) The five-element assessment-system abstraction — the architecture's headline component — arrives ex nihilo, unpositioned against evidence-centered design (Mislevy), the NRC assessment triangle, and G-theory's facet decomposition; its genuine addition (the assessor/system-under-test split and level-boundary localization) should be stated against those frameworks. (iii) The **organizational diagnosis/OD tradition**: Levinson's *Organizational Diagnosis* (1972) transplanted the clinical organization-as-patient method decades ago; Weisbord's Six-Box Model (1976) is a brief multidomain diagnostic framework (the coincidence of "six" invites the comparison); the §4.5 results-return-to-unit commitment restates the survey-feedback tradition (Mann 1957; Nadler 1977) without citation. (iv) **Theory-borrowing/analogy methodology** (Whetten, Felin & King 2009; Gentner 1983 structure-mapping; Morgan's organism metaphor; McKelvey 1999) and the **family-systems-applied-to-organizations prior art** (Friedman 1985; family business systems theory, Gersick et al. 1997). (v) **Gap-claim near-misses** (see §10 below), including — per fact-check — McKinsey's own *OHI Pulse*, a recurring short-form layer that partially contradicts the claim that the comprehensive frameworks' "use model" is "periodic in-depth assessment, not recurring screening."

**G. Internal-consistency repairs.** *(All verified.)* (i) §4.4 flatly asserts "Its items are unit-referent" two paragraphs after admitting they mix referents — repair the present-tense sentence and define the unit of inference (the items currently reference self, team, "my area," management, and the organization; "organizational unit," "intact units," and "systems engineering organizations" coexist unresolved). (ii) Table 4 row 1 asserts dense coupling is shared by "all three levels" while Limitation 5 treats it as an unverified presumption — make these consistent, engage loose coupling (Weick 1976) and near-decomposability (Simon), and extend §3.1's epistemic split to three tiers (individual indicator-sampling: supported; organizational dense coupling: hypothesis; organizational early warning: hypothesis). (iii) §4.3's "referent-shift consensus… is precisely the measurement model that family instruments employ" is false as written — family instruments supply the *wording* half of Chan's model; the consensus-aggregation half is organizational machinery, as §4.3's own opening sentence ("handled informally… since formalized") correctly says. (iv) §3.3's claim that multi-informant discrepancy analysis "already exists in validated form" conflicts with §4.3's "handled informally" — reconcile. (v) The agreement/dispersion fallback needs a falsification envelope: pre-specify which domains should show cross-role convergence vs. patterned divergence and state what pattern would count *against* the architecture, since §5.3 currently predicts both outcomes and the paper stakes its own standard on "the precision of the predictions it licenses."

## 6. Theoretical coherence: the person–family–organization CAS ladder

The as-CAS discipline of §2.1 is the right standard and the property-by-property transfer rule is sound. Three problems, all repairable. First, the **"nested levels / two single-level steps" framing** trades on a vertical metaphor the ontology does not support: persons nest in both families and organizational units, but the three systems do not form one compositional hierarchy. The substantive argument already matches the modest version (the Figure 1 caption already locates the family rung's epistemic work at the person-to-multi-actor transition; §4.3 openly names its two precedent sources), so the fix is terminological plus a clarifying sentence — but as written, "nested" is false and "two single-level steps" presupposes a level metric the paper defines only qualitatively. Second, the **dense-coupling warrant** is internally inconsistent (asserted as shared in Table 4 and §5.1; conceded as presumption in §2.5 and Limitation 5) and unengaged with the organization-theory literature most threatening to it (Weick 1976 loose coupling; Simon near-decomposability). The strongest available repair is disjunctive: under dense coupling, few indicators suffice; under modular coupling, per-module sampling is required — multidomain sampling is robust across both regimes, which is a *stronger* design argument than the current one. Third, the **family-theory-to-CAS mapping** (Table 2), which the paper calls "the first load-bearing element of the cross-level argument," is loose at two joints (second-order change ≠ critical transition in the formal sense; Bowen triangles ≠ agent heterogeneity) and elides the cybernetics/GST-vs-complexity distinction. The argument needs only that family theory developed structural analogs of feedback, emergence, and reorganization — which is true — not that it anticipated complexity science specifically. Reframe "anticipated" accordingly.

Verified positive: the two-evidence-line structure (structural + empirical-by-precedent), the §3.1 epistemic split, and the hedged organizational early-warning treatment are coherent and consistently maintained.

## 7. The family-systems intermediate-level argument

The family scholarship is accurate at the level presented (fact-checked: Jackson, Minuchin, Bowen, Watzlawick, Cox & Paley, Olson, SCORE all check out; the 1983 FAD item count does not — 53 items, not 60). The intermediate-level argument earns its keep as an **existence proof**: family assessment demonstrates that brief, multidomain, multi-informant, system-referent, repeatable measurement of an emergent relational construct is achievable and psychometrically defensible — something neither the clinical nor the team literature supplies. What it does not earn is the **convergent-evolution induction** as stated (see concern A) or the claim that the family tradition exhibits the *pairing* element "point for point": the GF/SCORE-15 relations are short-form derivations, not independent-comparator validations, and no GF-triggers-full-FAD triage pathway exists in family practice. The five anchor-selection criteria in §3.3 are openly derived from the architecture's needs (legitimate functional-requirements reasoning), but criterion 5's "including for the calibration uses described in Section 5.2" imports the authors' own simulation program into the definition of a good intermediate level — cut that clause or mark criterion 5 as a program-specific desideratum, and report that the family-vs-teams outcome is unchanged under criteria 1–4 (it is, by the paper's own analysis). Also acknowledge that the celebrated epidemiological GF uses were typically single-informant: no single family use-case combines brief + multi-informant + repeated + comparator-paired — the OHA proposes the first *conjunction*, which is honest and actually sharpens the contribution claim. Finally, the discrepancy-as-information precedent rests on clinical-interpretive practice (Miller et al. 2000) plus an individual-referent informant literature (Achenbach; De Los Reyes & Kazdin); the directly relevant parent–adolescent FAD discrepancy literature exists and would serve the paper better.

## 8. Psychometric and multilevel measurement claims

The citation base is correct and current (Chan, Kozlowski & Klein, Morgeson & Hofmann, James, Bliese, LeBreton & Senter, Putnick & Bornstein, Kane, the Standards), and deploying Kane at the development stage is sophisticated and legitimate. The "validity does not transfer" line is exactly right and consistently held. Four repairs: (1) the **referent-shift attribution** (concern G.iii) — one sentence, but it is the sentence that underwrites "the hard transitions are already solved," so it matters; (2) the **aggregation gap** — consensus-justified aggregation to a unit score is performed at neither precedent rung; the paper's own two-source story (family precedent + organizational multilevel theory) handles this honestly elsewhere, so split the Table 4 multi-informant row into administration-with-discrepancy (family precedent, adapts) vs. consensus-justified aggregation (organizational-literature precedent) and the architecture's completeness claims survive in honest form; (3) the **falsifiability envelope** for the agreement/dispersion logic (concern G.v) — as specified, high agreement validates the aggregate and low agreement becomes an informative dispersion finding, with role-dependent item functioning likewise "plausible and, where found, informative"; Chan is explicit that dispersion constructs must be specified a priori, and the paper already grounds one (candor gradients via Edmondson) — what is missing is domain-level designation and a stated disconfirmation condition; (4) the **small-N program** (concern C), which is the single largest technical gap because the paper's claimed contribution *is* the measurement architecture.

## 9. The OHA application: does the architecture support it?

Partially, and the paper is unusually auditable about it — but the audit finds gaps. The six-domain derivation is "an argued choice, not an inheritance," yet no criterion explains why the "organization" pathology category splits into two domains while "knowledge and understanding" (loaded with role clarity, goal alignment, *and* knowledge sharing) does not, and the correspondence table (Table 5) and the domain table (Table 6) carve the construct space differently with no crosswalk (FAD "Roles" → role clarity/decision rights in one; role clarity under Knowledge and Understanding in the other). The McMaster burden-envelope sentence should go (citation mismatch). The chronology (OHA predates the architecture) is disclosed twice, and the referent-shift self-critique genuinely rebuts the pure post-hoc charge — but the paper never performs the audit that would convert the circularity objection into evidence of generativity: which carr2025 features the architecture *ratifies*, which it *revises* (referents — given; agreement reporting, dispersion interpretation, governance, and the §4.3 evaluation plan appear to be post-carr2025 additions but the paper never says so), and which it would have *prohibited*. A short delta table would do it. The simulation plan's staged logic is genuinely novel and the family rung does indispensable work there; its two unaddressed threats are training-data contamination of the known answers and the (correctly grammatically scoped, but rhetorically ambiguous) fact that family-level calibration certifies only the *transferring* capabilities, not role-graded candor or hierarchy effects. Governance is strong on candor, identifiability, and purpose drift but omits the *mirror-image* distortion — Goodhart/Campbell reactivity under consequential repeated administration (score inflation, item learning, campaign timing) — and the legal-institutional layer (works councils, GDPR, union consultation, security constraints in the SE sector), plus who controls administration timing when leadership is itself the dysfunction.

## 10. Missing literature, unsupported claims, and citation needs

**Counterexamples/near-misses the gap claim must engage** (the claim is hedged as "open to counterexample" — these are the counterexamples): Gallup Q12 (Harter, Schmidt & Hayes 2002), Safety Attitudes Questionnaire (Sexton et al. 2006), AHRQ HSOPS (Sorra & Dyer 2010), COPSOQ short form (Kristensen et al. 2005 — brief-plus-comprehensive pairing *within* COPSOQ), HSE Management Standards Indicator Tool (Cousins et al. 2004), DEOCS command climate surveys, and McKinsey's own OHI Pulse. Most fail on system-referent emergent-health grounds, dispersion-as-signal, or within-tradition comparator pairing — say so, instrument by instrument, and restate the gap in the narrower defensible form. These also force corrections to §3.3's "research findings rather than clinical-grade infrastructure" (HSOPS/SAQ have benchmark databases) and §5.2's "no published organizational benchmarks exist."

**Literatures to add:** multilevel homology (Chen, Bliese & Mathieu 2005; also Rousseau 1985 and Klein, Dansereau & Hall 1994 for the levels framing); climate strength as the organizational instantiation of dispersion composition (Schneider, Salvaggio & Subirats 2002); organizational silence/voice for the candor gradient (Morrison & Milliken 2000; Detert & Burris 2007); multisource/360 feedback for role-stratified informants (Conway & Huffcutt 1997); OD diagnosis (Levinson 1972; Weisbord 1976; Burke & Litwin 1992; Harrison & Shirom 1999) and survey feedback (Mann 1957; Nadler 1977); theory borrowing/analogy (Whetten et al. 2009; Gentner 1983; Morgan 2006; McKelvey 1999); family-organization prior art (Friedman 1985; Gersick et al. 1997; Casey 1999 for the family-metaphor caution); peer-reviewed organizational health and construct differentiation (Wilson et al. 2004; DeJoy & Wilson 2003; Denison 1996; Schneider, Ehrhart & Macey 2013; Patterson et al. 2005 OCM — also a candidate validated component for the OHRA); construct-coherence caution (Cameron 1986); network-theory critics (Fried & Cramer 2017; Forbes et al. 2017; Bringmann & Eronen 2018); pre-1983 screening tradition (Goldberg 1972; Beck 1961; Zung 1965); family-side primary sources (Byles et al. 1988; Ridenour et al. 1999 and the McMaster exchange; Jackson 1965; Bateson 1972; Watzlawick, Beavin & Jackson 1967); failure incubation (Turner 1976/1978).

## 13. Final recommendation

**Major revision.** The paper makes a defensible scholarly contribution — the family-instrument precedent, the element-by-element transfer discipline, the staged calibration design, and the governance treatment are real and, in combination, novel — and its claim discipline is well above the genre norm. No fatal flaw was found: every major concern has a concrete, identified fix, and the modest version of the thesis survives all of them. But the headline claims (independent re-emergence; nested levels; hardest transitions already solved; the OHRA as the pattern's third comparator; family instruments employing referent-shift consensus) each currently outrun what the body establishes, the measurement plan omits the reliability half of aggregation and the small-N regime it will actually face, several boundary literatures (homology, OD diagnosis, near-miss instruments, theory borrowing) are unengaged, and the bibliography contains errors (including an apparently fabricated author list on a load-bearing citation) that must be corrected before any submission. Conditions of acceptance: items A–D of Section 4, plus the homology and near-miss-instrument engagement and the hardness-ordering correction. With those revisions, the strongest venue fit is a methods-oriented organizational journal (e.g., *Organizational Research Methods*) after compressing Sections 2–3 and deepening Section 4, or a systems venue (*Systems Engineering*; *Systems Research and Behavioral Science*) if the family-science exposition is retained at current length.
